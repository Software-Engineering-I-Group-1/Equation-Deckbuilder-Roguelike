Project Description

Decrementor is a web-based roguelike that combines deckbuilding mechanics with algebraic problem-solving. Players build and refine a deck of three card types: numbers, unary operator modifiers, and binary operators. At the start of the game, players' decks contain basic single-digit numbers, fundamental operators such as addition and subtraction, and simple modifiers such as negation. During encounters, players must form valid equations from cards in hand to meet specific requirements to damage the enemy. Successfully defeating each enemy rewards players with choices of new cards—larger numbers and more advanced operators. As the game progresses, players must use strategic deck management and creative equation building to overcome increasingly difficult challenges.

Background

Decrementor was developed to create an engaging and educational game experience for middle school students (grades 6–8). The project blends core algebra skills with the replayability of roguelike games. The game starts simple and gradually introduces concepts such as exponents, prime numbers, and the Fibonacci sequence. This progression helps players consolidate prior knowledge and build new mathematical understanding.

Original Project Description

As video games become increasingly more common, teachers and parents are increasingly using them to supplement their children’s educations in a fun way. Educational games may improve student engagement and incentivize learning. In the past, games such as the Oregon Trail, Number Munchers, SimCity, the Dr. Brain series, and Mavis Beacon Teaches Typing have all been used to either teach or reinforce ideas.

Your team will design and prototype a new educational video game. This game must be suitable for students between 4th to 8th grade (late elementary school or middle school). You may choose to narrow the target age range. The target demographic will likely have an effect on the text, concepts, and mechanics that you want to use; Young children may not have the reading, motor, or analytical skills required for some designs.

The game must have an educational component. The Massachusetts Department of Education and Common Core standards can give some suggestions for topics that are appropriate for different age levels.

This project is open-ended. You have freedom to choose the language, engine, genre, and topic for your game.
